October-December 1995

Highlights

During October, November and December of 1995, the following features were noticeable:
  • Dry conditions over northeast China in October while the warm spell over southeast Africa ended abruptly. Wet weather dominated over eastern US, western Europe was mild (October, November).
  • In the northern Atlantic drifting buoys continued being caught in small scales oceanic eddies (November, December), the longest trajectories being found at 15°N between 120°E and 150°E. During December long trajectories were also found between 5°N-5°S east of 150°W. This is also the area where the easterlies picked-up.
  • The SOI is slightly near zero during October and November.
    Standardized SOI
  • Sea level anomalies (pressure corrected) were 10 cm above normal at 15°S between 170°E and 170°W.
  • Sea surface temperature anomalies (SSTA) were above normal in the North Pacific, with 1°C anomalies along the US west coast and from the sea of Okhotsk to the Kamchatka peninsula. Negative SSTA were present in the Niño 3 and Niño 4 regions which continued the trend of the last period.
    NINO3 (degreeC)
  • Positive SSTA persisted in the western and subtropical Atlantic. Positive SSTA in the path of African easterly waves contributed to a very active Hurricane activity in the Atlantic (Gray et al., Wea. Forecasting, 1993a and b). 19 tropical storms and 11 hurricanes were counted at the end of October. These were the second largest numbers since 1886! Typhoon Zack and super Typhoon Angela were extremely destructive over Vietnam and the Philippines. Heavy rain returned to Indonesia.
  • At the end of the period the largest negative SSTA are found in the central north Pacific around 40°N and the southwestern southern Atlantic around 50°S. The SST along the eastern USA is colder than normal.
  • The thermocline continue to get deeper (+20m) in the western equatorial Pacific west of 170°E) and shallower (-40m) around 130°W and the equator during November.
    IGOSS leetma depth of the 20C isotherm (m)
All of the above conditions clearly described a weak La Niña period, in spite of a still slightly negative SOI during December. An important feature is the persistence of the SSTA in the northern subtropical and eastern Atlantic which could be associated with low frequency gyre variability there.
